🎤 Spoken version S/T ~30s · Action ~70s · Result ~30s · Reflection ~15s

“The hardest feedback I've gotten felt unfair when I heard it — and it was the most useful. My manager told me I was complaining about a broken relationship instead of fixing it. He was more right than I wanted him to be.”

Situation / Task

I led 40-plus engineers at TeamCity. Our relationship with the product team — five PMs under a peer Head of Product — was strained: I'd send clear engineering needs, get little back, and end up making product calls I wasn't the right person to make. I was loud about that upward. Around May 2025 my manager came to me and said: instead of building the product relationship, I was just complaining it was broken. It stung — my engineers saw how hard I was working at it, and days earlier we'd had a genuine breakthrough with product on our AI strategy. Part of me felt mislabeled. My task was to get past that and figure out what was actually mine to fix.

Action

I sat with it for two days instead of arguing back, and made myself debug my own behavior, not just the situation. The uncomfortable finding: the feedback I'd been giving upward was symptoms, not solutions — “this is broken” gave my manager and the Head of Product nothing to act on, and they already knew. I'd told myself I was respecting their authority by not reaching into their teams — which is real — but I'd let it slide into passivity.

Then I looked at my own track record: where had engineering and product actually worked well? The wins had a pattern — PMs seconded into my team, explicit rules of engagement agreed up front that protected their relationship with their own boss but built in engineering's interests, and me stepping in fast when we drifted. So I changed what I brought into the room: I stopped escalating complaints and started bringing that mechanism. I reproduced it deliberately on other projects — friction dropped each time — and over a few months turned it into a framework the whole department used.

Result

Two things told me it stuck. A developer who'd been the most skeptical about product ended up pushing to bring PMs into a feature discussion himself. And from Q4 2025 we shipped zero features without an engineer–PM pairing — collaboration became the default. My manager acknowledged the turnaround in our year-end review. It compounded: in Q1 2026 we reorganized into cross-functional stream teams with PMs embedded in each business unit — and it went through with almost no friction, because the relationship was already real.

Reflection

In a conflict there's rarely a single right person — and even when you're objectively right, your job is to resolve it in the healthiest way, not to be proven right. My failure mode was naming what was broken instead of owning the fix, and calling my own passivity “respect.” Now, when I catch myself complaining about the same thing twice, I treat it as a sign I haven't built the system yet.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

What was your first reaction — honestly?

Frustration, and a feeling it was unfair — we'd literally just had a breakthrough with product. I spent two days replaying it before responding. That gap is where I got past “I'm wronged” to “what's actually mine here.”

Wasn't your manager just wrong — wasn't it really the PMs' fault?

That's the trap I had to climb out of. The PM org had its own gaps — but the only lever I controlled was me, and I'd been spending it on complaints instead of a mechanism. Blaming them would've just proven my manager's point.

What exactly did you change, and how do you know it stuck?

I stopped bringing problems upward and started bringing a tested framework — PMs embedded, rules of engagement agreed up front, fast course-correction. Proof: a PM-skeptic developer started inviting PMs in, and from Q4 2025 every shipped feature had an engineer–PM pairing. The stream-team reorg went through frictionlessly off the back of it.

You didn't want to overstep the PMs' authority — isn't a department-wide framework exactly that?

The opposite. It was co-designed with the PMs and explicitly protected their relationship with their Head of Product — their roadmaps, their focus, their reporting line. I wasn't redirecting their people; I was giving both sides agreed rules so engineering's needs were heard without me playing shadow-PM.

What feedback do you still watch for?

Over-correcting — pulling PMs so deep into execution that engineers lose focus. I treat the engineering↔product balance as something I manage continuously, not a problem I closed.

Delivery notes:
  • The whole story lives or dies on NOT sounding like “my manager was wrong and the PMs were bad.” Concede the kernel early and sincerely.
  • The conflict-maturity reflection is the line that wins the room — land it slowly.
  • Numbers: 40+ engineers, Q4-2025 zero features without pairing, Q1-2026 frictionless reorg.

🎤 Spoken version S/T ~25s · Action ~80s · Result ~25s · Reflection ~15s

“I led a project to build a converter migrating existing GitHub Actions and Jenkins pipelines into TeamCity — it stalled at PoC and got shut down. Two of my own calls drove that, and they shared one root: I read the warning signals too late.”

Situation / Task

We were making a bet on adoption: a converter that turns existing GitHub Actions YAML and Jenkins Groovy pipelines into TeamCity's Kotlin DSL — so the pitch becomes “even with complex CI/CD, you can migrate easily.” The core product hypothesis: only AI could do that conversion. I led a cross-functional team of six — two backend, frontend, QA, a PM — accountable for the full cycle: PoC, MVP, EAP, release.

Action

My first call was team composition, and it cost me. For the key engineering seat I picked a senior with deep system knowledge — even though my manager warned me, and my own instinct agreed, that his mindset was more corporate than startup. This was a team that needed to build fast and kill hypotheses, sometimes throwing work away. I overweighted expertise and underweighted mindset — and brought him in anyway.

The second was an omission. I didn't set up facilitation at the start — honestly, I lacked both the skill and the willingness to get my hands into it. I left the team to agree on milestones themselves, and they didn't: no product vision, no formalized backlog, no user stories. The signal showed up two weekly syncs in a row — blockers from engineering one week, product the next. They weren't resolving dependencies; they were passing the ball at a one-week interval. I stepped in to facilitate — but by then trust had already eroded into a standoff.

There was a technical layer too, with an honest nuance. The AI bet itself was deliberate — testing and killing hypotheses is the job, not a failure. The failure was that code is a deterministic domain and AI hallucinates, so it needed guardrails guaranteeing the output compiles — and every iteration revealed the next step needed nonlinearly more effort. That was an early “stop and rethink” signal. Instead I kept feeding it.

Result

We did deliver a PoC, but the results were judged not good enough, and we stopped there — it never reached MVP. The cost was concrete: about two full-time people over roughly four weeks — call it eight person-weeks — went into opaque activity instead of delivery, and the team had reached a full interlock. Stronger bets emerged in the department around then, and we refocused onto those.

Reflection

Both failures had the same root — I saw the signals, the weekly ping-pong and the nonlinear effort curve, and acted late. So I changed two things. On every new team since, the initial phase is non-skippable: agreed ownership, end state, risks and stakeholders before any code. And on a bet, nonlinear effort growth is now a kill-or-pivot trigger, not a reason for one more iteration. The very next stalled PoC I took over, I opened with exactly that foundation — and the team shipped a demo in two weeks.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

What specifically was your fault?

Two of my decisions. First, I picked the engineer against my manager's warning and my own read — overweighted expertise, underweighted the mindset mismatch. Second, and more important, I never built the initial phase: no vision, no backlog, no agreed milestones. I handed that to the team instead of facilitating it. The idea and the market aren't the story — I didn't lay the foundation.

What did you do the moment you realized it was failing?

The trigger was two weekly syncs in a row with blockers on both sides — a pattern, not a one-off. I stepped in to facilitate and started with the most basic check: does a product vision and backlog even exist? They didn't. The real problem: I did this too late — the sides were already in interlock.

If the technical bet was deliberate, where's the failure?

Testing the hypothesis wasn't the failure. The failure was reading the signal late: code is deterministic, AI without guardrails can't guarantee compilation, and each next step cost nonlinearly more. That was an indicator to stop and rethink — I added resources instead.

What did you change, and did it work next time?

The initial phase became mandatory: ownership, end state, risks, stakeholders agreed before development. On the next stalled PoC I opened with exactly that — cleared the fog, pulled stakeholders together, broke the goal into components with milestones — and the team shipped a demo in two weeks.

Why did you pick that engineer against the warning?

The product was technically hard — parsing others' DSLs, generating Kotlin DSL — so deep system knowledge lowered technical risk. Real value, weighed wrong: for a team that must kill hypotheses fast, speed and willingness to throw work away matter more than depth of legacy knowledge.

Delivery notes:
  • Pick the spine to the probe: people/self-awareness → composition + facilitation; delivery/results → the AI bet, nonlinear curve, late kill. Don't tell both at full depth.
  • Say “I” on the failures: I picked him, I didn't facilitate, I read the signal late.
  • Never drift into “the market wasn't ready” — every cause points back to one of my decisions.

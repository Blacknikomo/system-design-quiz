“I drove a build across three separate JetBrains divisions where I only managed one of them — so I couldn't mandate anything; I had to make two other divisions want to stay aligned with the plan.”

Situation / Task

We set out to build an end-to-end agentic workflow spanning three divisions: our issue tracker, our CI/CD platform — my area — and our AI-agent team. The flow: a user opens a ticket, hits “solve” right in the tracker UI, that fires a job on CI, which launches the AI agent against the repo to attempt the fix. The hard part was organizational: three divisions, three roadmaps, three sets of priorities — and I had authority over exactly one. My job was to deliver my department's piece and keep the whole thing aligned, without being anyone's boss.

Action

I treated it as an influence problem, not a coordination problem. First I made the shared goal concrete and visible — a kickoff where all three sides agreed what “done” looked like and who owned what — because alignment is cheap when everyone sees the same target.

Then the real lever: I ran the business and product track two-to-four weeks ahead of engineering, so by the time engineers hit a milestone we'd already de-risked the direction and rough design. Early on some people read that as bureaucracy — my overhead. I didn't argue; I let it prove itself. Within two weeks it caught a blocker that would've stalled everyone: my team hit a release freeze from a security audit and literally couldn't ship the API the other teams needed. Instead of letting them stall or forcing my team to break freeze, I proposed putting the unstable API on a dedicated staging server — unblocking the other divisions without violating our freeze. After that, the “bureaucracy” framing died; the skeptics started bringing their own blockers to the same forum.

The hardest person to align was the lead on the AI-agent side — a brilliant engineer, full of ideas, who kept wanting to spin off quick experiments mid-build. I didn't shut him down — that kills both the relationship and the ideas. I channeled it: grooming became the place ideas got pressure-tested before any code. We killed a few expensive detours early, and a couple of his hypotheses turned into real features.

Result

The clearest proof the alignment worked: across the whole two-month build — three divisions, about twelve people — total blocked time was roughly two days. We shipped a working internal PoC and demoed it to select customers. We later shelved the PoC itself — agentic systems weren't good enough yet — but it directly seeded our next initiative: an Agents Hub that five of us carried forward, since grown into a real product of around eight people. The thing I was really building — alignment across orgs that don't report to me — outlived the project.

Reflection

When you're influencing across orgs, the cheapest way to keep three teams aligned isn't more process — it's getting ahead of the work, so the inevitable blockers surface while there's still room to trade around them.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

You didn't own two of the three teams — why did they follow your process at all?

I never asked them to follow my process — I asked them to hit a shared goal and made my process the cheapest way to get there. The proof point was the staging-server save: once the two-weeks-ahead rhythm prevented a real blocker, staying aligned became self-interested, not a favor to me.

What did you give up to get alignment?

Two things. I ran the lightest-possible project management — only the minimum that earned its keep — because anything heavier would've confirmed the “bureaucracy” read. And I gave up some of my own team's capacity: a couple of my engineers were effectively full-time on this — a real cost to our own roadmap that I owned.

Who pushed back hardest, and how did you win them?

The AI-agent lead. He wasn't resisting the goal — he was over-generating ideas that risked derailing the build. Winning him wasn't overruling him; it was giving his ideas a real home. Grooming became the filter: he got heard, we avoided expensive detours, and some of his hypotheses became features.

What would you do differently?

Protect my own team's identity and capacity harder up front — at one point they started identifying as a new unit rather than TeamCity. And staff the system-design-critical seats more carefully: a brilliant mid-level coder without deep design experience cost us a few redone decisions — caught early only thanks to the ahead-of-engineering rhythm.

Delivery notes:
  • Keep foregrounding “I couldn't mandate, so I…” — the moment you say “I organized syncs,” you sound like a coordinator.
  • Numbers: 3 divisions ~12 people, ~2 months to PoC, ~2 days total blocked, Agents Hub 5 → ~8.
  • Say “I” on: the staging-server idea, running business ahead of eng, making grooming the filter. Say “we” on the build itself.

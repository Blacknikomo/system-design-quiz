🎤 Spoken version S/T ~30s · Action ~75s · Result ~25s · Reflection ~10s

“TeamCity was hitting a hard performance ceiling on a 20-year-old architecture, and “performance” was an internal swamp nobody wanted to own. I set the direction — a horizontal-scale redesign earned through safe incremental wins — tied it to a real business risk, and turned a track people avoided into the roadmap we still run today.”

Situation / Task

TeamCity is a 20-year-old CI product, built to scale vertically — one instance handled around 10,000 simultaneous jobs. Customers wanted well beyond that, and AI-generated code was driving load up fast — a ceiling on a core product promise. Two problems made it hard. First, no agreed direction: incremental optimization and a full horizontal redesign were both on the table and nobody would commit. Second, performance had a terrible internal reputation — a bottomless swamp of micro-work nobody got credit for, with a default belief that the existing architecture was “good enough.” My job: set a credible technical direction and get both the team and leadership to invest — without gambling the stability thousands of customers rely on.

Action

I refused to let it stay a binary. I spent two days going deep into both architectural proposals myself, because I wanted to set direction on the technical merits, not from the org chart. The thesis I landed on: the only way to truly clear the ceiling is a horizontal-scale redesign — multinode, DB sharding, caching — but on a product this many customers depend on, we earn the right to that redesign through incremental, rollback-safe wins first. Long-term bet, de-risked path.

Then three things to make it real. I tied it to business impact, not engineering taste — the job ceiling plus AI-driven load growth was a competitive and revenue risk, and that framing is what unlocked multi-quarter investment with PMs and leadership. I made the direction decidable instead of opinion-driven, with a shared scoring framework — impact/effort, time, rollback safety, complexity — so every bet was judged the same way. And to win the skeptics, I reframed the track from “swamp” to high-risk, high-reward modern systems work, gave it real ownership, and ran a week-long offline gathering where the team itself turned the direction into scored Epics — so they owned the roadmap, not me.

Result

That framework and those Epics became the performance roadmap we still run today — a multi-quarter direction, not a one-off. The track flipped reputation: from something people avoided to a high-reward area that attracts strong engineers. And it's already paying off concretely — early work caught a TLS issue on the server-to-database layer and cut P99 latency from 6.5 seconds to 1 second.

Reflection

I almost lost the room by leading with the engineering case. A multi-quarter architectural bet gets funded on a business frame — the ceiling as competitive and revenue risk — not on architectural elegance. Translate the bet into the language of whoever controls the investment before you walk in, not halfway through the meeting.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

How did you know it was the right direction?

I didn't trust my gut — I made it decidable. Deep dive into both proposals, then every option through the same weighted criteria: impact/effort, time, rollback safety, complexity. The direction fell out of the criteria, not from whoever argued hardest. And I validated the business premise with PMs and customers — the demand past the ceiling was real.

Why a hybrid and not just commit to the redesign?

A pure redesign on a 20-year product with thousands of customers is how you ship an outage and lose trust. A pure optimization track can't clear the ceiling — diminishing returns. The hybrid banks safe wins now — which also fund credibility for the bet — while building the architecture that removes the ceiling.

How did you bring along the skeptics?

Two moves. I reframed the work's status — from thankless swamp to the most technically interesting, high-leverage area we had — backed with real ownership and a roadmap. Then I let the team author the Epics at the gathering instead of handing down a plan. The reputation flip was the proof: people started wanting onto the track.

How did you balance it against short-term delivery?

That tension was the design. The rollback-safety and impact/effort criteria deliberately front-loaded fast, low-risk wins — like the P99 6.5s→1s fix — so we shipped visible value every quarter while the bigger pieces matured. Short-term delivery funded the long-term bet's credibility instead of competing with it.

What would you do differently?

Lead with the business case from the first sentence. I opened on architecture and nearly lost the PMs and leadership; investment unlocked only once I reframed the ceiling as competitive and revenue risk. Now the money-and-market framing comes first, the technical plan follows.

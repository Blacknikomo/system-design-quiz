🎤 Spoken version S/T ~30s · Action ~70s · Result ~30s · Reflection ~10s

“I took a mid-level engineer who was quietly running our most critical new subsystem and, over about eight months, grew her into its Senior-level owner — which also let me remove myself as the bus factor.”

Situation / Task

We were building the TeamCity AI Assistant — a brand-new subsystem that had to become a first-class citizen inside a 20-year-old product. I stood up a cross-functional team of six and was explicitly accountable for two things: delivering it, and guaranteeing its technical excellence — which meant staying deep in the code and process only until the team could run without me. One engineer stood out — I'll call her V. On paper a mid-level SWE, but she understood the system deeply, worked proactively, and was effectively driving its evolution — senior signals. The gap was real though: she'd get stuck in endless re-planning, was shaky on our UI patterns, didn't facilitate meetings with confidence, and under-invested in stakeholder work. My task: grow her into the subsystem's accountable owner — which is how the team becomes autonomous and I stop being the single point of failure.

Action

In a 1:1 I named it directly: “On the technical side you're already operating like a senior, and I believe you can own this subsystem — which means a promotion. Here's the gap, and I'll help you close it.” The gap wasn't coding — it was scope-through-others: moving from pure IC to technical leader. Underneath, a habit carried from being mid-level — used to working under the provisioning of senior leads.

So I made the nudge structured, not chaotic. We built a SMART career plan with checkpoints on top of regular 1:1s. I brought her directly into stakeholder and Product discussions and coached her through them. I shared the frameworks I use — a mix of PMI and Google PdM practices tuned for TeamCity — plus the department's goals and the why behind our decisions, so she could navigate ambiguity herself instead of escalating to me. Where she flagged her own gaps — frontend patterns, Product alignment — I set up cross-workshops between frontend, QA and backend. Throughout, I deliberately stayed a coach, not a mentor: I didn't hand her answers, because the whole point was that the team kept running when I stepped back.

Result

Over about eight months, it landed. Promo conversation opened in April; she took about two months to tune her work to the Senior bar, sustained it for five — and was promoted, recognized by the team as its technical lead. By then she owned the SDLC and the core system decisions, and I'd dropped to occasional coaching. On delivery: the PoC shipped a month ahead of plan, the release hit its ETA, and plan-vs-actual stayed within statistical noise. Autonomy and on-time technical excellence — both landed, and the bus factor was gone.

Reflection

My honest mistake: I poured all of this into one person, and for the other five it was fairly opaque — it could have read as favoritism. Now I set shared growth expectations with the whole team up front and make the path visible, so it builds collective trust instead of a private track.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

What was your contribution vs. hers?

I created the conditions; she did the work. I spotted the potential, built the promo path, sponsored and coached — deliberately as a coach, not a mentor, so the team kept running when I stepped back. The engineering, the ownership, and the authority she earned are hers. I won't claim those.

What was the specific gap, and how did you close it?

Over-planning instead of committing, shaky UI patterns, unconfident facilitation, under-invested stakeholder work. Root: a mid-level habit of working under someone else's provisioning. Levers: SMART goals + checkpoints to force decisions; cross-workshops for UI patterns and shared vision; direct stakeholder exposure with me coaching, then stepping back.

How did you make the case at calibration?

Evidence, not advocacy. A significant over-contribution performance mark, strong 360s, and hard delivery metrics — zero schedule slip, plan-vs-actual within statistical noise. I compiled it and was explicit these were her achievements; my job was the rigor.

Anyone you couldn't grow — why?

Yes. The common pattern is loss of sustained focus: ambitious at the start, then drifting back into operational work, re-engaging only at year-end review — too late to build a case. Lesson: I re-test commitment at every checkpoint, not annually, so a fading signal surfaces early.

🎤 Spoken version S/T ~25s · Action ~80s · Result ~30s · Reflection ~10s

“Two hires, both technically excellent — one became one of my best hires ever, the other cost my team the equivalent of a senior engineer. The difference wasn't the technical signal; it was how rigorously I de-risked the non-technical one.”

Situation / Task

Two roles where I was the hiring manager. First: TeamCity had systemic performance problems — every engineer was strong individually, but almost nobody looked at performance systemically, with fresh eyes. I made the case to my manager to hire a high-level engineer who would eventually lead that direction. Second, in parallel: a mid-level IC for the Build Tools Integrations team. My bar for both: high-load experience outside TeamCity — a fresh view — and the ability to drive change politely but persistently, because we'd be changing the architecture of a 20-year-old product. My hard no-hire: a non-growing mindset — someone who doesn't try to understand another point of view and declares there's only one right way.

Action

The win — D, a Staff-level candidate. Technically he answered better than anyone before him: deep system design, strong coding, fluent across domains — frankly better at engineering than me, which I took as a good sign. But we'd deliberately built some questions around suboptimal “colleague” decisions and system constraints — and there D lit up with engineering excitement and forgot facilitation: interrupting, not hearing people out. A direct risk against my bar. I neither rejected him nor looked away. I requested an extra round where I named the risk explicitly: you'll work with a team that's been building this product for 20 years, many decisions will look suboptimal, and you'll need to challenge them respectfully. I showed him the specific moments from the interview that could read badly — and listened. He acknowledged the tendency and explained he'd switched into “partners, not interviewers” mode. Then I proposed an explicit contract: he accepts ongoing feedback from me on soft skills, and I carefully navigate him through the relationship landscape. Risk named → coachability tested → risk managed.

The miss — M, a mid-level IC. The interview went well; technically strong. Along the way I learned something important: he'd never worked in high-ambiguity teams. We talked about it, discussed how my team works, and “agreed it would be fine.” That was my mistake: the exact same class of risk — non-technical fit — but I accepted a verbal agreement as mitigation instead of testing evidence or building a plan. The strong technical signal made me underweight it.

On the job it showed: M needed heavy management — at times two of my engineers spent up to half their time on his onboarding. I gave feedback; it improved. Then the department transformation raised the seniority bar. I waited to see whether the improvement was durable — it wasn't. So I made the call and had an honest conversation: in the new structure his skills didn't meet expectations. Because the bar had moved unexpectedly for both sides, I didn't just manage him out — I prepared a supporting reference and helped him land in another JetBrains team with less ambiguity.

Result

D delivered from day one on data: met every stakeholder, collected their problems, built a roadmap. Within months he'd instrumented performance metrics — REST, DB queries, agent load — profiled the product and found critical issues: for example, TLS handshakes on DB connections eating up to 80% of REST request time, which he fixed. Together we created the new Performance direction that led to a serious architecture rethink — DB sharding, multi-node, log processing. One of my most productive hires. M: we parted correctly and he landed well; the cost — up to one senior FTE in aggregate on prolonged onboarding and management attention.

Reflection

In both cases the deciding variable wasn't the technical signal — it was non-technical fit in a technically strong candidate. The difference was the rigor of de-risking: with D I named the risk, tested coachability, and managed it with a contract; with M I accepted a verbal “we agreed” as mitigation. Now I test non-technical risk on evidence, not promises — past behavior, scenarios — and if risk remains, an explicit de-risking plan or early onboarding checkpoints with a short feedback loop.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

What's your bar — what's a no-hire for you?

The hard no-hire is a non-growing mindset: someone who doesn't try to understand another point of view and declares there's only one right solution. For these roles it's critical — you can't change a 20-year-old architecture without hearing the team. Role-specific: high-load experience outside our product, and driving change politely but persistently.

What signal did you misread on the miss?

Not technical — he was fine there. He told me directly he'd never worked in high ambiguity. I didn't miss the signal — I devalued it, accepting a verbal “it'll be fine” as mitigation against a strong technical signal. I weighed it wrong.

Why hire the Staff candidate with an explicit red flag?

Because it was a calculated, de-risked bet, not a hope. Three things: an exceptional technical ceiling; the flag proved coachable — he acknowledged it rather than denying it, the opposite of a non-growing mindset; and I had a concrete management plan — the extra round plus the feedback contract. With the miss, none of that plan existed — only a promise.

How do you handle a borderline candidate?

The Staff hire was borderline — on soft skills. I didn't guess or reflex-reject: I converted the uncertainty into a test (an extra round surfacing the exact moments) and into managed risk (the contract). Borderline means: probe it with a targeted round, or hire with an explicit mitigation plan — never on vibes.

Did you act fast enough on the miss?

Partially no, and that's an honest zone. Feedback came on time and improvement started, but I judged its durability against a bar that the transformation had just raised, and I waited longer than needed. Today I'd set early onboarding milestones with a shorter feedback cycle — the signal would have surfaced sooner.

What did you change in your hiring process?

Three things: non-technical risk — especially ambiguity-handling — is tested as past behavior and scenarios, not “will you cope?”; if risk remains in a strong candidate, an explicit de-risking plan like the contract; and early onboarding milestones with a short feedback loop to catch mismatch in the first months.

🌐 Spanner & NewSQL — global SQL that doesn't lose your money

TrueTime, commit-wait, Paxos under every split — how Google made SQL span continents, and how CockroachDB, TiDB & Aurora attack the same problem without atomic clocks. ☕ ~15 min read
external consistency TrueTime Paxos per split 2PC over Paxos interleaved tables NewSQL

Spanner is the database Google built when sharded MySQL stopped being survivable: relational, ACID, SQL — and sharded, replicated and writable across regions, with the strongest consistency guarantee in the industry. The unlock wasn't a cleverer algorithm alone; it was hardware honesty: put GPS receivers and atomic clocks in every datacenter, admit exactly how wrong any clock can be (ε), and make every commit wait out that doubt. A few milliseconds of deliberate waiting turn timestamps into something you can trust to order the whole planet's transactions.

In the interview: in a Google loop, Spanner is "the strongly-consistent global OLTP option" — say it when the requirements demand multi-region writes, no lost updates, and real SQL transactions (payments, ledgers, inventory, bookings). Expect follow-ups on: why waiting makes clocks safe (TrueTime + commit-wait), what multi-split transactions cost (2PC over Paxos), why keys must not be monotonic (hot split), and what you'd pick outside GCP (CockroachDB & friends).

1 · The mental model: sharded Postgres where time is an API

Start with the pain. Google's ads business — the revenue engine — ran for years on a manually sharded MySQL fleet. The Spanner paper mentions, almost deadpan, that the last resharding of that system took over two years of careful, risky effort. That one sentence is the entire pitch. Sharding a relational database by hand buys you scale and quietly repossesses everything else: cross-shard transactions become your problem, global secondary indexes stop existing, resharding becomes a multi-year migration, and failover becomes a runbook full of "should be fine". Spanner (built over roughly five years, published at OSDI 2012, later sold as Cloud Spanner) is Google refusing that trade.

The storage layout is the easy half. A table lives sorted by primary key and is chopped into splits — contiguous key ranges. Each split is its own Paxos group: several replicas spread across zones (or regions), one of them elected leader. Writes for a split go to its leader and commit once a majority of replicas have them; splits split, merge and move around automatically based on both size and load. So far this is "sharded Postgres with self-driving shards and consensus instead of primary/replica" — nice, not revolutionary. Transactions that touch one split are just a Paxos round. The hard problems are the other two: transactions across splits, and consistent reads across a database whose thousands of leaders each march to their own clock.

Here is why clocks are the deep problem. Suppose you commit T1 ("deposit €100") on node A, and — after A confirms — a colleague commits T2 ("spend €100") on node B. If B's clock runs 5 ms behind A's, B may stamp T2 with an earlier timestamp than T1. Nothing crashes. But any snapshot read, backup, or auditor replaying history in timestamp order now sees the spend before the deposit that funded it — money from nowhere. Ordinary serializability doesn't forbid this; it only promises some consistent order, not one that matches wall-clock reality. Spanner promises the stronger thing — external consistency: if T1 completes before T2 begins, T1's timestamp is smaller. Full stop, planet-wide.

The trick is a chain of notary offices. Every notary's watch may be wrong, but never by more than ε — and each notary knows its ε. Protocol: stamp each document with your watch plus ε (the latest the true time could be), then hold the document on your desk until your watch minus ε has passed the stamp. Only then hand it back. That pause guarantees that by the time anyone sees a stamped document, its stamp is already in the past for every notary on Earth — so two documents notarized one after another, even in different cities, can never carry inverted stamps. That is TrueTime (an API that returns an interval [earliest, latest] instead of a lying single number) plus commit-wait (the deliberate pause). GPS receivers and atomic clocks exist purely to keep ε small — single-digit milliseconds — so the pause is cheap. And if the clock hardware degrades, ε inflates and Spanner gets slower, not wrong.

Serializable vs externally consistent — the one-sentence test. Serializability: "there exists some serial order consistent with what everyone saw." External consistency (≈ linearizability for transactions): "that serial order also matches real time — if your commit finished before mine started, yours sorts first." Spanner gives the second; most databases (and most NewSQL competitors) give only the first.

2 · Syntax & core API

Cloud Spanner speaks GoogleSQL (a PostgreSQL-dialect interface also exists). The schema keywords below are where the distributed design leaks into the SQL — each one is worth being able to explain, because each is a physical-placement decision wearing DDL clothes.

PRIMARY KEY (…)Defines physical sort order and split boundaries. The one rule: never monotonic (no timestamps, no sequences) — all inserts would land on the last split's leader. Use UUIDv4, a bit-reversed sequence, or a hash prefix.
INTERLEAVE IN PARENTPhysically stores child rows inside the parent's key range → a customer and their orders live in the same split, so "checkout" stays a single-split (no-2PC) transaction.
CREATE INDEX … STORING (…)Secondary index — itself a distributed table with its own splits (global by default; can be interleaved). STORING copies columns in to make it covering; a non-covering index read is a second, cross-split lookup.
read-write transactionPessimistic: acquires locks at each involved split's leader (wound-wait deadlock avoidance), commits via Paxos — plus 2PC if several groups are involved.
read-only transactionLock-free. Picks a snapshot timestamp, reads a consistent view across the whole database. Never blocks writers, never aborts them — Spanner keeps multiple timestamped versions of each row.
stale read (bounded / exact)"Give me data as of ≤ 10 s ago" — can be served by the nearest replica with no leader round-trip. The latency escape hatch for read-mostly, geo-distributed traffic.
allow_commit_timestampColumn option; write PENDING_COMMIT_TIMESTAMP() and Spanner fills in the TrueTime commit timestamp — changelogs and sync feeds ordered by the real commit order.
SEQUENCE (bit-reversed)Built-in sequence that hands out bit-reversed integers: unique, roughly uniform over the keyspace, hotspot-safe — sequential IDs without the sequential-key penalty.
-- Schema that keeps checkout single-split and keys hotspot-free
CREATE TABLE Customers (
  CustomerId STRING(36) NOT NULL,        -- UUIDv4, NOT a timestamp
  Name       STRING(MAX),
  CreatedAt  TIMESTAMP OPTIONS (allow_commit_timestamp = true)
) PRIMARY KEY (CustomerId);

CREATE TABLE Orders (
  CustomerId STRING(36) NOT NULL,
  OrderId    INT64 NOT NULL,
  Total      NUMERIC,
  Status     STRING(16)
) PRIMARY KEY (CustomerId, OrderId),
  INTERLEAVE IN PARENT Customers ON DELETE CASCADE;   -- orders live WITH their customer

CREATE INDEX OrdersByStatus ON Orders(Status) STORING (Total);  -- covering → one lookup

-- Reads: three consistency dials, three prices
SELECT * FROM Orders WHERE CustomerId = @id;   -- strong read: sees every commit that finished before it started
-- client option: read-only txn { exact_staleness: 15s }  → served by nearest replica, no leader hop
-- client option: read-only txn (strong, multi-table)     → one snapshot timestamp across ALL splits, lock-free

3 · Use cases — when Spanner is the answer

🧾 Financial ledgers & payments
Double-entry ledger, users on three continents, two writable regions for survivability. External consistency means the replay of history in timestamp order is the truth — an auditor never sees a spend before its funding deposit, a backup restored at timestamp T is a consistent cut of the world.
Why Spanner: "multi-region, both writable, zero lost updates" is precisely the requirement classic replication can't meet honestly.
📦 Global inventory, bookings & ticketing
Last seat on the flight, buyers in Tokyo and Berlin at the same instant. Read-write transactions lock the row at the split leader; one buyer commits, the other cleanly retries. No oversell, no "eventual" apology emails.
Why Spanner: contention on single hot rows is exactly what pessimistic locks + Paxos handle predictably.
🌍 User identity & control planes for multi-region products
Accounts, entitlements, configuration — data every region must read fast and any region may write. Strong writes go through leaders; the read fleet uses bounded-staleness reads from the nearest replica for local-latency reads that are still never-older-than-10s consistent.
Why Spanner: the staleness dial gives you per-query choice between "correct now" and "fast here".
🔁 The escape hatch from sharded MySQL / Vitess
You sharded, it worked, and now cross-shard transactions, global indexes and the next resharding own your roadmap (Google's own story — F1/AdWords migrated ads off sharded MySQL onto Spanner). NewSQL is that operational debt, refinanced: resharding becomes automatic split movement.
Why Spanner: it was literally built as this migration's destination.

When Spanner is the WRONG answer

single region, modest scaleA boring Postgres (or Cloud SQL / AlloyDB) is cheaper, richer in extensions, and has no commit-wait or Paxos tax. Distributed SQL for a single-region CRUD app is résumé-driven design — say so in the interview.
analytics / OLAPHuge scans and aggregations want columnar engines (BigQuery, ClickHouse). Spanner is row-oriented OLTP; ship changes out via CDC instead.
sub-ms reads / cache dutyCommit-wait + consensus put a physics floor under latency. Point-read-heavy serving at microsecond budgets → Redis / DynamoDB+DAX in front.
write firehose, no transactionsClickstreams, telemetry, feeds: you're paying for consistency you don't need — Bigtable / Cassandra take the writes for far less.
tight budgetPriced per node-hour + storage, multi-region configs a multiple of regional. The floor for "toy project" money is high; the value only shows up when the requirements are cruel.

4 · Distributed setup — what "running it for real" means

You don't operate Spanner; you configure it. A regional instance puts 3 read-write replicas in 3 zones of one region: quorum round-trips are intra-region, so writes land in single-digit-to-low-tens of milliseconds. A multi-region config spreads replicas across regions and adds two special citizens: a default leader region (put it where your writers are — every read-write transaction talks to leaders) and witness replicas, which vote in Paxos but don't store full data — quorum durability without paying for another full copy. The write path is then: client → split leader → cross-region Paxos majority → commit-wait → ack; latency is dominated by the RTT to the nearest quorum, tens of milliseconds and up, set by geography rather than software.

Reads are where the config earns its keep. Strong reads must prove freshness — served from the leader, or from a replica after it confirms it's caught up past the read timestamp, which can mean a leader round-trip. Stale reads skip all of that: any nearby replica answers from its history, and TrueTime timestamps make "at most 10 s old" an actual guarantee rather than a hope. Well-built Spanner apps are loud about this split: strong reads on the money paths, bounded staleness everywhere else.

Where it sits in CAP: Spanner is honestly CP — in a partition, the minority side stops accepting writes; consistency is never traded. And yet Eric Brewer's 2017 paper argues Spanner is "effectively CA": on Google's private, redundantly-pathed fiber backbone, partitions are so rare that observed availability exceeds five nines, so users are entitled to act as if it were CA. Quote that carefully in an interview — it's a claim about measured availability on Google's network, not an exemption from the theorem. Formally: CP, with availability high enough that nobody builds a fallback.

The two self-inflicted wounds. (1) Hot split: a monotonically increasing key (timestamp, sequence) aims every insert at the tail split — one Paxos leader becomes the whole database's throughput; load-based splitting can't fix a hotspot that's always at the newest key. Fix at schema time: UUIDv4 / bit-reversed sequence / hash prefix. (2) Fat transactions: every extra split in a read-write transaction adds a 2PC participant; a "batch update everything" transaction across dozens of splits turns commit into a cross-region committee meeting. Keep transactions few-split by design — interleaving exists precisely for this.

5 · 🎛 Play with it — TrueTime commit-wait, or: why waiting fixes clocks

Two split leaders with skewed clocks. You commit T1 on node A; after its ack, a client immediately commits T2 on node B (2 ms think-time). T2 is later in real time — external consistency demands its timestamp sorts later too. Without commit-wait, each node stamps with its own (wrong) clock. With commit-wait, each node stamps TT.now().latest and holds the ack until the stamp is provably in the past (~2ε). ε here equals the skew bound — TrueTime being honest about its uncertainty.

What to try

  1. Skew ±3, commit-wait off → Run. T2 (later in real time) gets the earlier timestamp — any snapshot between the two sees the spend without the deposit.
  2. Slide skew to 0, still off → Run. Perfect clocks need no tricks — the whole problem is skew.
  3. Skew ±5, commit-wait on → Run. Each node stamps latest, then sits out ~2ε (progress bar). Order is provably correct — the price is latency.
  4. With commit-wait on, slide skew 7 → 1 and re-run: the wait shrinks with ε. Better clocks ⇒ faster commits — that is why Google bought atomic clocks.
  5. Skew ±1, off → Run. No violation: the 2 ms gap happened to exceed 2·skew. Violations are rare races — which is why they're so nasty in production.
🔍 Deep dive · TrueTime mechanics — ε, the sawtooth, and the 2ε wait click to expand

TrueTime's API is a confession: TT.now() returns not a number but an interval [earliest, latest] guaranteed to contain true absolute time; the half-width is the uncertainty ε. The machinery behind it: each datacenter runs time master machines — some GPS-disciplined, some with atomic (armageddon-resistant, as the paper cheerfully calls them) clocks, deliberately different failure modes. Every server's time daemon polls a mix of masters, rejects outliers, and between polls (~30 s) inflates ε at a conservative assumed drift rate (~200 µs/s). So ε breathes as a sawtooth — in the 2012 paper, roughly 1 to 7 ms, averaging ~4 ms; modern deployments are said to be tighter.

Commit-wait, precisely: the leader picks commit timestamp s ≥ TT.now().latest, then blocks the visibility of the commit until TT.now().earliest > s — i.e., until every clock on Earth agrees s is in the past. Expected wait ≈ 2ε (assign at now+ε, wait until now−ε passes it). The elegant part: the wait overlaps Paxos replication, which also takes milliseconds — so much of the tax hides inside latency you were paying anyway. After that, external consistency is a two-line proof: T2 starts after T1's commit became visible ⇒ true time at T2's start > s₁ ⇒ T2's timestamp (≥ its node's latest ≥ true time) > s₁.

Notice the failure philosophy: Spanner never needs clocks to be right, only ε to be an honest bound. Clock trouble ⇒ masters get evicted, ε balloons, commits wait longer — graceful slowdown instead of silent corruption. The one unforgivable failure is ε being a lie (true time outside the interval); the redundant masters and paranoid drift assumptions exist to make that essentially impossible.

🔍 Deep dive · Transactions — 2PL for writes, timestamps for reads, 2PC over Paxos click to expand

Read-write transactions are old-school pessimistic: reads and writes acquire locks in the in-memory lock table of each involved split's leader (wound-wait avoids deadlock: an older transaction wounds a younger lock-holder instead of queueing forever). Single split ⇒ commit is one Paxos write plus commit-wait. Multiple splits ⇒ two-phase commit where every participant is a Paxos group. That sentence defuses the classic 2PC horror story: textbook 2PC blocks forever if the coordinator dies holding everyone's locks — but here the coordinator's state is itself replicated, so when the coordinator leader dies, a new leader is elected, reads the prepare/commit outcome from its group's log, and finishes the protocol. 2PC's fragility was never the two phases; it was the single point of memory. Paxos removes it. The cost that remains is honest latency: prepares to every participant leader, each a consensus round — which is why schema design (interleaving!) fights to keep transactions single-split.

Read-only transactions touch none of that. Spanner is a multi-version store — every write is stamped with its commit timestamp — so a read-only transaction just picks a timestamp (for strong reads, TT.now().latest) and reads the versions below it. No locks taken, no writers blocked, no aborts; your midnight analytics scan and your checkout path never notice each other. Each replica tracks a t_safe ("I have everything up to here") and can serve any read at t ≤ t_safe locally — this is the machinery that makes bounded-staleness reads from the nearest replica both fast and actually-correct, and it's TrueTime timestamps that let replicas make that promise without phoning the leader.

🔍 Deep dive · NewSQL without atomic clocks — CockroachDB, TiDB, Yugabyte click to expand

CockroachDB is the "Spanner outside Google" answer, and its central compromise is the clock. No GPS/atomic hardware — just NTP plus a configured max clock offset (default 500 ms) and hybrid logical clocks (physical time + logical counter). Instead of commit-waiting on every write, Cockroach makes reads pay: a read that encounters a value stamped within its uncertainty window can't tell "past or future?", so the transaction performs an uncertainty restart — it retries at a bumped timestamp. Same correctness goal, opposite billing: Spanner taxes every commit a few predictable ms; Cockroach taxes the unlucky reads with retries and tail latency. Data lives in ranges (~512 MB) replicated with Raft; a leaseholder serves reads; isolation is serializable by default (a rarity), and it speaks the Postgres wire protocol. Note the fine print: it guarantees serializability, not Spanner-grade external consistency across the board.

TiDB splits the stack: stateless SQL nodes over TiKV (RocksDB-based key-value store, Raft-replicated ~100 MB regions), with a Placement Driver issuing timestamps from a central TSO — timestamp oracle. One clock for everyone means no uncertainty dance at all — and a round-trip to the TSO on transactions plus a strong gravitational pull toward one region. It's MySQL wire-compatible (the migration story), commits via Percolator-style 2PC, and bolts on TiFlash columnar replicas for HTAP. YugabyteDB rounds out the field: it reuses the actual PostgreSQL query layer on top of a Raft-replicated document store, with HLC-based timestamps — the "we want real PG compatibility, distributed" bet.

The taxonomy to carry: consensus-replicated shards are table stakes (Paxos/Raft everywhere); the differentiator is where timestamps come from — hardware-bounded uncertainty (Spanner), uncertainty-with-retries (CockroachDB, Yugabyte), or a central oracle (TiDB). Every global-SQL design pays the clock tax somewhere; the only question is which line of the bill it appears on.

🔍 Deep dive · Aurora is not distributed SQL — and why interviewers probe this click to expand

Amazon Aurora gets name-dropped next to Spanner constantly, and conflating them is a classic interview trap. Aurora is a single-writer, scale-up MySQL/Postgres whose genius lives one layer down: compute and storage are split, and the storage tier keeps 6 copies across 3 AZs, acknowledging writes at a 4-of-6 quorum. The instance ships only redo log records — "the log is the database" — storage nodes materialize pages themselves, which slashes write amplification and makes crash recovery nearly instant. Up to ~15 read replicas hang off the same shared storage, so replica lag is milliseconds and failover is a promotion, not a rebuild.

But notice what it is not: writes still funnel through one writer node — there is no horizontal write scaling, no multi-region strong writes (cross-region replicas are async DR), and SQL throughput tops out at the biggest instance money rents. That's the boundary to say out loud: Aurora scales the storage under one SQL brain; NewSQL scales the brains. When a single writer's ceiling or multi-region writability is the requirement, Aurora graduates to Spanner/CockroachDB territory (AWS's own answer is bolting sharding back on — e.g. Aurora Limitless — which is Vitess-shaped, with sharding's usual asterisks). Until that moment, Aurora is often the better answer: one-region ACID with zero distributed-systems tax.

⚖️ At scale — numbers, limits & failure modes

Ballparks for capacity talk — quote orders of magnitude, not digits.

TrueTime εSawtooth ~1–7 ms, avg ~4 ms in the 2012 paper (30 s master polls + assumed drift); commit-wait ≈ 2ε, largely overlapped with Paxos replication. Treat "a few ms of commit-wait" as the quotable figure.
write latencyRegional config: single-digit to low-tens ms (intra-region quorum). Multi-region: tens of ms and up — floor set by RTT to the nearest quorum of regions, i.e., by geography.
per-node ballparkOrder of ~10k simple reads/s and a few thousand writes/s per node (small rows), several TB of storage per node — size instances on both throughput and data. Scale by adding nodes; splits rebalance automatically.
the unit of scaleA split has one leader — per-split write throughput is bounded no matter the node count. Hot single rows/keys don't shard away; that's a schema problem, not a capacity problem.
availability SLACloud Spanner: 99.999% multi-region, 99.99% regional — the five-nines number is the backbone of the "effectively CA" argument.
transaction limitsCommits have size/mutation-count caps — bulk backfills must be chunked. Long-running read-write transactions invite lock contention and aborts; do big reads as read-only (lock-free) transactions.
Failure modes that only appear at scale: tail-split hotspot (monotonic keys → one leader takes 100% of inserts while 99 nodes idle); 2PC fan-out (a transaction touching 30 splits pays ~30 prepare legs across regions — batch jobs written like OLTP melt); leader-geography mismatch (default leader region in the US, writers in Asia → every commit swims the Pacific twice); strong-read stampede (everything reads strong "to be safe" → leader-bound read load; bounded staleness exists for a reason); cost cliff (multi-region configs cost a multiple of regional — consistency you don't need is money you don't have).

8 · In the interview

How it comes up: rarely "explain Spanner" — usually a design whose requirements quietly demand it: global payments, worldwide inventory, multi-region active-active with zero lost updates. The senior move is naming the requirement that forces it: "we need multi-region write availability with transactional integrity — that's distributed SQL territory: Spanner on GCP, CockroachDB elsewhere." Then pre-empt the follow-ups: how it's consistent (TrueTime + commit-wait, external consistency), what it costs (commit latency ≥ quorum RTT + ~2ε, and money), and the schema rules (non-monotonic keys, interleave to keep transactions single-split).

The choice script: start with regional Postgres until a requirement kills it. Need read scale → replicas/caches. Need one-region write scale → Aurora-class or sharding (Vitess). Need multi-region writes + transactions → Spanner / CockroachDB / TiDB — and in a Google interview, know Spanner's mechanics cold, because the follow-up will be "and why does the waiting help?"

9 · Check yourself

Why does Spanner deliberately make every commit wait, and what exactly is it waiting for?

The leader stamps the transaction with TT.now().latest — the latest true time could possibly be — then holds the commit's visibility until TT.now().earliest exceeds that stamp: until every clock on Earth agrees the timestamp is in the past (~2ε, a few ms). After that, any transaction that starts later must receive a larger timestamp, so timestamp order = real-time order (external consistency). No waiting ⇒ a node with a slow clock could stamp a later transaction earlier, and snapshots/backups would see effects before causes.

Interviewer: "CAP says you can't have consistency and availability. How does Spanner claim both?"

It doesn't, formally — Spanner is CP: during a partition the minority side refuses writes. Brewer's argument is that on Google's private, redundant network, partitions are rare enough that measured availability exceeds 5 nines, so it's "effectively CA" — users don't build fallbacks. The theorem holds; the claim is about how rarely you pay the P. Bonus point: when clocks (not networks) misbehave, Spanner degrades to slower, never to inconsistent — ε grows and commit-wait grows with it.

Your orders table's primary key starts with created_at. What happens at 50k inserts/s, and what are the fixes?

Every insert targets the tail of the keyspace → one split → one Paxos leader takes the entire write load while the rest of the fleet idles; adding nodes changes nothing, and load-based splitting can't help because the hotspot is always the newest key. Fixes: UUIDv4 primary key, a bit-reversed sequence, or a hash/shard prefix (keep created_at as a regular column or index it separately). Same disease as monotonic partition keys in DynamoDB/Cassandra — different costume.

How does CockroachDB stay correct without atomic clocks — and where does that cost show up?

Hybrid logical clocks plus a configured max clock offset (default 500 ms). A read that meets a value stamped inside its uncertainty window can't tell whether that write is past or future, so it performs an uncertainty restart — retrying at a higher timestamp. Spanner pays a small, predictable tax on every commit (commit-wait ~2ε with ε in single-digit ms); Cockroach pays an unpredictable tax on reads (retries, tail latency) that grows with real skew. Same theorem, different victim.

When is Aurora the better answer than any distributed SQL — and what's the tell that you've outgrown it?

When writes fit one (large) writer and users are effectively one-region: you get ACID Postgres/MySQL, ms-lag read replicas on shared storage, fast failover — with zero distributed-transaction tax and no schema landmines. The tells you've outgrown it: the writer is vertically maxed, or the business demands multi-region writes with no lost updates — async cross-region replicas can't deliver that. Then it's Spanner/CockroachDB, and you accept quorum-RTT commit latency as the price of the guarantee.

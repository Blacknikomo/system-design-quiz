📊 ClickHouse & OLAP — columns, not rows

Why analytics engines shred tables into columns, how MergeTree turns disks into scan machines — and the price tag: batch inserts, eventual dedup, no transactions. ☕ ~15 min read
columnar MergeTree sparse index vectorized / SIMD eventual dedup scatter-gather

ClickHouse answers one question brutally well: aggregate a few columns over billions of rows, sub-second. It wins by tearing up the row-store contract: data lands in immutable sorted parts, every column in its own compressed file (~10× smaller is typical), scanned in CPU-friendly blocks and skipped granule-by-granule via a sparse index. The price is the contract's other half: inserts want to be batches, UPDATE is a background rewrite, dedup is eventual, and transactions essentially don't exist. OLAP isn't a faster OLTP — it's a different machine.

In the interview: reach for ClickHouse when you hear "analytics dashboard over billions of events", "logs & traces at scale", or "slice-and-dice, sub-second". The follow-ups are predictable: why columns beat rows for scans (I/O math), how data gets in (Kafka → batched inserts → parts & merges), why updates are awkward (immutable parts), and when you'd rather say BigQuery or Pinot (ops vs QPS).

1 · The mental model: per-field ledgers, not filing cabinets

A row store is a filing cabinet of order forms: each folder holds one complete order — id, customer, price, country, and 96 other fields. Ask it "average order value in Germany?" and a clerk must pull every folder, flip to the price box, glance at the country box, and put it back. The disk doesn't care that you wanted two fields; it reads pages, and pages hold whole rows. A column store is what an accountant would build instead: cut every field off the forms and paste them into per-field ledgers — one thin ledger of nothing but prices, one of countries. The same question now reads two thin ledgers and ignores the cabinet. And because a ledger of only prices is monotonous ("4.99, 5.99, 4.99…"), it compresses absurdly well — same-typed neighbors are a compressor's dream. Two columns of a hundred, then ÷10 compression: the analytics query just got orders of magnitude cheaper before any clever code runs.

ClickHouse is this idea taken to its logical extreme, born at Yandex.Metrica — a web-analytics product counting on the order of tens of billions of events a day — and open-sourced in 2016. The second trick it stacks on top: vectorized execution. Instead of interpreting a query row by row (the classic Volcano-style executor), it processes each column in blocks of tens of thousands of values — tight loops over homogeneous arrays that compilers and CPUs turn into SIMD, with cache lines fully used. Columns don't just shrink I/O; they make the CPU work the way CPUs like to work.

The storage engine family is called MergeTree, and it's an LSM-tree's cousin: every INSERT writes a brand-new immutable part — a directory with the batch's rows sorted by the table's ORDER BY key, one compressed file per column. Background threads continuously merge small parts into bigger ones (hence the name). There is no B-tree with point precision anywhere; instead a sparse primary index keeps one entry per granule (~8192 rows), tiny enough to live in RAM, good enough to decide which granules to scan and which to skip. Partitioning (typically by month) prunes whole chunks of data and makes retention trivial — dropping a partition, or letting a TTL expire it, is a directory delete, not a million DELETEs.

Everything strange about ClickHouse follows from those choices. Inserts should be big batches — each insert is a part, and ten thousand tiny parts drown the merger. UPDATE/DELETE are mutations — background jobs that rewrite entire parts. Uniqueness isn't enforced — ReplacingMergeTree deduplicates at merge time, eventually. It's the mirrored trade of the OLTP world: Postgres protects every row's mutability and pays for it on scans; ClickHouse sacrifices row-level mutability and gets scan speed that looks like a typo.

Row vs column — the one-sentence test. Many small reads/writes of whole rows (get user, update status) → row store. Few huge reads of few columns (aggregate 2 fields over a year) → column store. If a design needs both, it's two systems and a pipeline — HTAP databases exist, but the physics keeps pulling the two layouts apart.

2 · Syntax & core API

It's SQL — with a dialect that leaks the storage engine, which is exactly what makes fluency here read as hands-on experience. The engine clause and ORDER BY are where the design interview happens.

ENGINE = MergeTreeThe workhorse. Inserts create immutable sorted parts; background merges consolidate them.
ORDER BY (a, b, …)The schema decision: physical sort order = what the sparse index can skip by. Put your always-filtered, lower-cardinality columns first (tenant_id, toStartOfHour(ts), …).
PARTITION BYCoarse chunks (usually toYYYYMM(ts)): pruning, cheap DROP PARTITION, TTL unit. Don't over-partition — parts multiply per partition.
TTLRow/column expiry or move-to-cold-volume, executed during merges: retention as a schema property, not a cron job.
ReplacingMergeTree(ver)Dedup by sort key at merge time — eventually. Until a merge (or FINAL), duplicates are visible. There are no unique constraints, period.
Summing / AggregatingMergeTreeMerges fold rows with the same key: pre-aggregated rollups maintained by the storage engine itself.
MATERIALIZED VIEWAn insert trigger: transforms each inserted block into a target table (usually a rollup). Incremental, at ingest — not recomputed, and not retroactive.
ALTER TABLE … UPDATE / DELETEMutations: asynchronous background rewrites of whole parts. For fixing data, not for OLTP. (Lightweight DELETE marks rows and cleans up at merges.)
SELECT … FINALForce merge-semantics (dedup/fold) at query time. Correct and expensive — a smell if it's on every dashboard query.
async_insertServer buffers many small inserts into one part — the escape hatch when clients can't batch.
dictGet('dim', 'attr', id)In-memory dictionaries for dimension lookups — the idiomatic replacement for star-schema joins.
uniq / quantile / topK / argMaxApproximate & positional aggregates: HyperLogLog-style distincts, sketch quantiles, "value at max(ts)" for latest-row-wins queries.
-- events table: the ORDER BY is the whole design review
CREATE TABLE events (
  ts       DateTime,
  site_id  UInt32,
  user_id  UInt64,
  country  LowCardinality(String),
  price    Decimal(18,2)
) ENGINE = MergeTree
PARTITION BY toYYYYMM(ts)                        -- prune by month; TTL drops are directory deletes
ORDER BY (site_id, toStartOfHour(ts), user_id)   -- a tenant's dashboard reads a contiguous slice
TTL ts + INTERVAL 90 DAY;

-- rollup maintained AT INSERT TIME (target: AggregatingMergeTree, read with -Merge)
CREATE MATERIALIZED VIEW events_hourly_mv TO events_hourly AS
SELECT site_id, toStartOfHour(ts) AS h,
       count() AS views, uniqState(user_id) AS users
FROM events GROUP BY site_id, h;

-- typical interactive query: 3 columns, billions of rows, sub-second
SELECT toStartOfHour(ts) AS h, uniq(user_id), quantile(0.95)(price)
FROM events
WHERE site_id = 42 AND ts >= now() - INTERVAL 7 DAY
GROUP BY h ORDER BY h;

3 · Use cases — when ClickHouse is the answer

📈 Product & click analytics
One wide events table — page views, clicks, feature usage — and questions like funnels, retention, "DAU by country by hour". Billions of rows, queries touching 3–5 columns, dashboards that must feel instant to the analysts hammering them.
Why ClickHouse: this is literally the workload it was built for at Yandex.Metrica; wide-table + sub-second aggregation is home turf.
🪵 Logs & traces (observability)
Structured logs at millions of rows/s. Compression makes retention affordable, columns make "error rate by service by minute over 30 days" a scan, not an archaeology dig. Cloudflare and Uber both run huge log pipelines on ClickHouse — and half the new observability vendors are ClickHouse wearing a UI.
Why ClickHouse: aggregation-heavy log analytics beats inverted-index engines (Elasticsearch) on cost/compression by a wide margin; you trade away relevance-ranked text search.
📊 Real-time dashboards & ad-tech reporting
Advertisers slicing impressions by campaign × geo × hour, minutes-old data, seconds-old freshness. Materialized views fold events into per-hour rollups at ingest; dashboards hit the rollup, ad-hoc questions hit the raw table.
Why ClickHouse: ingest-time pre-aggregation (Summing/AggregatingMergeTree) gives dashboard latency without giving up raw-data flexibility.
🚰 The classic pipeline: Kafka → ClickHouse
Services emit events to Kafka; a consumer (or ClickHouse's own Kafka engine table + materialized view) writes batched inserts — tens of thousands of rows per INSERT. Kafka absorbs spikes and decouples producers; ClickHouse gets the fat batches MergeTree wants.
Why this shape: it solves ClickHouse's one ingestion demand (batching) with a component the design already has, and replays are possible when a table needs rebuilding.

When ClickHouse is the WRONG answer

OLTP / point updates"Set order #123 to shipped" — mutations rewrite parts asynchronously; there are no real transactions. State of record lives in Postgres/MySQL; ship events to ClickHouse.
high-QPS point reads"Get user profile by id" at 20k QPS — the sparse index finds granules, not rows; every lookup decompresses ~8k-row granules per column. Use a row store / KV.
frequent tiny insertsOne row per request = one part per request = merge death ("too many parts"). Batch upstream, or async_insert — and if you can't, reconsider the store.
strict integrityPayments, balances, inventory: no unique constraints, no foreign keys, no multi-statement transactions. Wrong tool, full stop.
relevance text searchLog filtering is fine (token/ngram skip indexes), but ranked full-text search with scoring is Elasticsearch/OpenSearch territory.

4 · Distributed setup — replicas that swap files, shards that scatter-gather

Replication is per-table: ReplicatedMergeTree. Coordination runs through ClickHouse Keeper (a built-in Raft implementation, protocol-compatible with ZooKeeper, which it replaces). The mechanics are refreshingly physical: any replica can accept an INSERT (multi-writer); the new part is registered in the replication log in Keeper; the other replicas see the entry and fetch the part file from a peer. Replication ships parts, not statements — and it's asynchronous by default, so replicas converge rather than march in lockstep (an insert_quorum setting exists when you need an ack from several replicas). Merges are coordinated too, so replicas don't diverge on layout decisions.

Sharding is honest and manual: a Distributed table holds no data — it's a router. Queries fan out to every shard, each shard aggregates its slice, and the initiator merges partial states (scatter-gather; aggregation pushes down, so what travels is compact). Inserts route by a sharding key you choose. What you do not get: automatic rebalancing when you add shards (resharding OSS ClickHouse is a data-copying project), cross-shard transactions (none), or global secondary indexes. ClickHouse Cloud sidesteps much of this with SharedMergeTree — parts live in object storage, compute nodes are stateless-ish and scale independently — the same storage/compute separation idea BigQuery and Snowflake bet on.

Distributed gotchas interviewers fish for: a hot shard from a skewed sharding key (one giant tenant lands on one shard — hash by something with cardinality); JOINs across shards (a naive distributed join broadcasts or reshuffles — GLOBAL JOIN ships the right table to every shard; keep dimension data in dictionaries instead); and Keeper pressure — every table × partition × replica adds coordination metadata, so thousands of small tables/partitions grind the control plane. Design fat tables, few partitions, big parts.

5 · 🎛 Play with it — the row-vs-column scan race

One table, 100 columns. The analytics query SELECT avg(price) … WHERE country='DE' touches 2 of them. The row store must read whole pages — every column of every row; the column store reads two column files, compressed. Then flip the workload: a point lookup by id needs all columns of one row — and the race inverts. Bars are time-compressed to the loser's tape.

What to try

  1. 1 B rows, 10× → Run analytics: ~800 GB vs ~1.6 GB read — the whole columnar story in one race.
  2. Slide compression 1× → 20× and re-run: the column bar keeps shrinking; rows don't benefit — mixed-type pages barely compress.
  3. Drop to 1 M rows: both finish instantly. Under a few GB, Postgres is fine — columnar pays off when data ≫ RAM.
  4. 🔎 Point lookup: inverted — the row lives in one page (~4 reads) vs ~100 granule reads across column files. This is why OLTP serving stays row-based.
  5. Re-run the point lookup at 20× compression: barely moves — seeks and decompression dominate, not bytes.
🔍 Deep dive · Inside MergeTree — parts, granules, marks, and merge debt click to expand

A part is a directory: for each column, a compressed .bin file; a primary.idx holding the sparse index; mark files (.mrk) mapping index entries to byte offsets. The rows inside are sorted by the ORDER BY key, and the sparse index stores the key of every 8192nd row (one granule). Query planning is then beautifully dumb: binary-search the in-RAM index to find candidate granule ranges → read only those granules' byte ranges from only the referenced columns → decompress → vectorized scan. For columns outside the sort key, skip indexes (min-max, set, Bloom-filter) hang per-granule summaries on the data so whole granules can still be skipped without being read.

Every INSERT creates a new part, so a table is a forest of sorted runs, and background merges continuously k-way-merge them into fewer, larger parts — this is where ReplacingMergeTree dedups, Summing folds, and TTLs expire. The health metric of a ClickHouse cluster is therefore parts per partition and merge backlog: if inserts outpace merges, reads touch ever more parts and slow down, and past a threshold (classically delays around ~150 active parts per partition and rejects around ~300 — thresholds tunable and raised in newer releases) inserts start failing with the famous "Too many parts". It is not an error so much as the engine enforcing its contract: feed me batches.

That contract shapes the ingestion tier of every design: batch tens of thousands of rows per INSERT, let Kafka accumulate, or turn on async_insert so the server does the batching. And keep PARTITION BY coarse — partitioning by day × tenant multiplies the part count by tenant cardinality, and the merge scheduler drowns in confetti.

🔍 Deep dive · Where did my UPDATE go — mutations, ReplacingMergeTree, FINAL click to expand

Parts are immutable, so ALTER TABLE … UPDATE/DELETE can't touch rows in place — a mutation schedules a background rewrite of every affected part. It's asynchronous (the statement returns before the data changes), heavy (a one-row update can rewrite gigabytes), and queued behind merges. Lightweight DELETE improves the ergonomics — rows get masked and physically removed at merge time — but the honest rule stands: ClickHouse data should be modeled as immutable events, with correction as new data, not surgery on old data.

When you genuinely need "latest state per key" — say each order's current status — the idiom is ReplacingMergeTree(version): insert a full new row per change; merges keep the highest version per sort key. The catch every interviewer probes: dedup happens only when a merge happens, and merges are eventual and never guaranteed to have merged everything. Until then, queries see duplicates. Your options, worst to best: SELECT … FINAL (forces merge semantics at query time — correct, but you pay merge cost per query); argMax(col, version) with GROUP BY key (aggregation-time dedup, usually the right call); or making ingestion idempotent upstream so duplicates never enter. There's also CollapsingMergeTree, which cancels rows via +1/−1 sign pairs — powerful for keeping running aggregates correct, fiddly to feed.

Notice what this really is: the same LSM logic as Cassandra's tombstones or Kafka's compacted topics — correctness is defined at compaction time, observed lazily. Say the phrase "dedup is eventual; we design ingestion to be idempotent and read with argMax" and the follow-up questions dry up.

🔍 Deep dive · Materialized views — aggregation moved to insert time click to expand

A ClickHouse materialized view is not a cached query — it's an insert trigger. When a block of rows lands in the source table, the MV's SELECT runs on that block only and the result is inserted into a target table. Chain that with an AggregatingMergeTree target and partial-aggregate states (uniqState, sumState … read back with uniqMerge / sumMerge), and you get rollups that are incrementally maintained at ingest — the per-hour table your dashboards hit is always a few seconds behind the raw events, and querying it costs thousandths of the raw scan.

The architecture pattern: raw wide table (source of truth, 90-day TTL) → MVs fanning out to a handful of rollups shaped exactly like your dashboards (per hour × campaign, per minute × service…), each with longer retention and tiny size. Ad-hoc questions hit raw; products hit rollups. This is how ClickHouse serves "real-time dashboard" latencies without a separate serving layer.

Sharp edges to name: an MV sees only blocks inserted after it was created — backfilling means inserting old data through it or careful POPULATE-style rebuilds (racy against live inserts); MV logic runs inside the insert path, so a broken MV can fail or slow the source insert; and chains of MVs multiply that fragility. Keep MVs few, simple, and monitored.

🔍 Deep dive · The OLAP landscape — warehouses, real-time engines, embedded, lakehouse click to expand

"Analytics" hides four different products, and interviews reward knowing which one the requirements are pointing at. Cloud warehouses — BigQuery, Snowflake — separate storage from compute completely: data in object storage, stateless compute rented per query (slots/credits, or per-TB-scanned). Elastic to petabytes, zero ops, superb for internal analysts and ELT — with latencies in the seconds and a cost model that punishes high-frequency querying. Interactive OLAP engines — ClickHouse, Apache Druid, Apache Pinot — keep data hot and indexed for sub-second answers: ClickHouse is the general-purpose scan monster; Druid and Pinot (LinkedIn's "who viewed your profile") specialize in user-facing analytics — pre-aggregated, thousands of concurrent queries, more moving parts to operate.

Embedded OLAP — DuckDB — is a columnar engine inside your process, the "SQLite of analytics": perfect for single-node data science, Parquet crunching in a lambda, or an app feature; it simply isn't a shared server. And the lakehouse — Parquet/Iceberg files on S3 queried by Trino/Spark — is the cheapest possible storage with federation across sources, at the price of interactive latency (also the natural cold tier behind ClickHouse; CH reads Parquet/S3 directly).

The positioning sentence worth memorizing: ClickHouse sits between warehouses and serving engines — warehouse-scale scans at interactive latency, high-rate querying at fixed hardware cost — and you pay with cluster operations (or buy ClickHouse Cloud) and with OLTP-shaped features it deliberately lacks.

⚖️ At scale — numbers, limits & failure modes

Ballparks for capacity talk — quote orders of magnitude, not digits.

scan speedSimple filter + aggregation: hundreds of millions to billions of rows/s per node (order of ~10⁸/core). This single number is why "just scan it" replaces most indexing conversations.
compression~10× typical on event/log data (LZ4 default; ZSTD tighter); specialized codecs (Delta, DoubleDelta, Gorilla) do far better on timestamps/gauges. Quote "order of 10×".
index anatomyGranule = 8192 rows; one sparse-index mark per granule; the primary index lives in RAM. Point reads decompress whole granules — that's the OLTP tax.
ingestion rulesBatch 10k–100k+ rows per INSERT; keep insert frequency per table low (classic guidance: ~1/s) or use async_insert. Single nodes ingest hundreds of thousands of rows/s; clusters, millions.
parts healthActive parts per partition is the ops metric: delays kick in around ~150, rejects around ~300 (classic defaults; tunable, raised in newer versions). Watch merge backlog like you watch queue depth.
memoryGROUP BY / hash JOIN build in RAM — default per-query cap ~10 GB; big right-side joins OOM. Denormalize, use dictionaries, aggregate before joining. Spill-to-disk exists; it's a consolation prize.
concurrencyDefaults allow ~100 concurrent queries; the engine wants few heavy queries, not thousands of tiny ones. User-facing 1000+ QPS → rollups + cache, or Pinot/Druid.
Failure modes that only appear at scale: too-many-parts insert storm (trickle inserts or confetti partitioning → merge debt → rejected inserts); join OOM (hash-joining two fat tables at query time — the schema was supposed to be denormalized); mutation pileup (regular UPDATE/DELETE traffic queues rewrites behind merges until disks thrash); Keeper strain (thousands of tables × partitions × replicas of coordination metadata — consolidate tables); hot shard (sharding key = one whale tenant's id); FINAL everywhere (correctness crutch that silently multiplies every dashboard query's cost).

8 · In the interview

How it comes up: as the analytics half of almost any design — "design YouTube's view-count dashboards", "ad-click analytics", "log search for a platform", "metrics for 10k tenants". The senior shape is always the same split: OLTP store for product state; events to Kafka; OLAP store for questions — never analytics queries against the production Postgres. Then the follow-ups: how data gets in (batched, via Kafka; MVs building rollups at ingest), what the ORDER BY is (tenant first, time second — a tenant's dashboard reads one contiguous slice), and what happens at 10× (parts, merges, add shards — and name the resharding pain honestly).

The choice script: internal analysts, ad-hoc SQL, minimal ops → BigQuery/Snowflake and move on. Interactive product analytics / observability at high ingest with sub-second dashboards → ClickHouse (self-run or Cloud). Customer-facing analytics at thousands of QPS → Pinot/Druid. Tiny/embedded → DuckDB. Saying why the boundary is where it is — cost model, latency class, concurrency architecture — is the L6 signal.

9 · Check yourself

Why is SELECT avg(price) WHERE country='DE' orders of magnitude cheaper on a column store — three distinct reasons?

(1) I/O selectivity: it reads 2 column files of 100 instead of every page of every row — ~2% of the bytes before anything else happens. (2) Compression: same-typed adjacent values compress ~10× (order), so those bytes shrink again. (3) Vectorized execution: the survivors are processed as blocks of homogeneous values in SIMD-friendly loops rather than row-at-a-time interpretation. Multiply the three and 800 GB of logical data becomes ~a-second scan.

A service inserts one row per request, 5,000 times a second, straight into MergeTree. What breaks and how do you fix it?

Every INSERT creates an immutable part; 5k parts/s outruns merges → reads slow (each query touches thousands of parts), merge debt grows, and inserts start being delayed and then rejected ("Too many parts"). Fixes, best first: batch upstream (Kafka + consumer inserting 10k–100k rows), enable async_insert so the server buffers, and check the partition key isn't multiplying parts. The rule: ClickHouse eats batches, not rows.

Product wants "current status per order" in ClickHouse, with status changes streaming in. Options and their catches?

Insert a row per change into ReplacingMergeTree(version) — but dedup happens at merge time, eventually: plain queries see duplicates. Read with argMax(status, version) GROUP BY order_id (idiomatic), or FINAL (correct, expensive per query). ALTER … UPDATE is a background part-rewrite — fine for corrections, wrong for streams. And if this is really OLTP state used by the product, it belongs in Postgres, with ClickHouse holding the history.

Your analytics dashboard becomes a customer-facing feature at ~2,000 QPS of small queries. What's the problem, and the two escape routes?

ClickHouse is architected for few heavy queries (defaults near ~100 concurrent); thousands of small concurrent queries contend on scans that were never meant to be per-end-user. Route 1: stay on ClickHouse but serve from MV-maintained rollups shaped exactly like the widget, plus a cache — queries become tiny key-range reads. Route 2: move the serving workload to Pinot/Druid, which are built for user-facing concurrency. Naming the concurrency boundary is the senior signal.

Interviewer: "Why not just BigQuery for all of this?"

Match the tool to query economics and latency class. BigQuery: zero ops, elastic to petabytes, priced per scan/slots, latency in seconds — perfect for internal analysts and ELT. ClickHouse: sub-second interactive queries at high frequency on fixed hardware cost — perfect when dashboards are hammered continuously or are product features, and when ingest is a firehose you control. Many real designs use both: ClickHouse hot path, warehouse/lakehouse for everything-else analytics.

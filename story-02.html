“I built a brand-new cross-functional team for our biggest AI release — and within the first month I had to decide whether one engineer who disagreed with how the team worked could stay on it.”

Situation / Task

At TeamCity we were building AI Assistance — our biggest bet that year: combine the huge amount of data our CI/CD pipelines produce with AI summarization. That meant integrating a completely new subsystem into a twenty-year-old product, under real competitive pressure. I stood up a cross-functional team — senior engineers, QA, product, design. Within the first month I got consistent signals about one engineer, I'll call him S: reviews needing several rounds, peers telling me privately he wasn't acting on feedback and kept rewriting their code, product flagging that he polished existing code instead of building committed features. My job: figure out whether this was a skill, a motivation, or a fit problem — and either get him back to the bar or make a clean call. What I refused to do was quietly route work around him — unfair to the team and to him.

Action

First I separated facts from impressions — I pulled his review turnaround, defect rates, and delivery history from his previous team, so the conversation would be grounded. Then a direct but respectful 1:1: specific gaps with examples, questions rather than assumptions. What came out: S genuinely disagreed with how the team worked — from code style to product vision — and instead of raising it publicly, he quietly took it on himself to do things “the right way.”

So I did two things. We agreed on a 30-day plan with concrete, measurable goals: quality and ownership targets, weekly check-ins with me, a senior engineer paired on design reviews. I documented it, was explicit it was a real opportunity and not a formality — and equally explicit about what would happen if the bar wasn't met. Second, I opened a standing channel with product as a forum for exactly these strategy disagreements, so silent divergence had somewhere to go.

How it landed: S improved meaningfully on velocity and code quality — but not on the two things the role needed: design judgment and collaboration. He kept doing large refactors and steering effort away from the team's goals. At the midpoint I told him straight we were off track on what mattered most. By the end of the plan it was clear the role wasn't the right fit. I made the call to move him off the team — with dignity: a long runway, a strong internal reference for a role matching his strengths, full transparency with HR.

Result

Two outcomes, and I won't pretend it was clean the whole way. Those 30 days were turbulent — the team cheered when things improved, got frustrated when problems returned, and I was conscious I was a source of stress for S. But because the exit was handled cleanly, he transitioned to a team that fit him within a month — and last I heard, both he and that team were happy. On my side the backfill ramped fast: reviews stopped being about code style and started being about design, delivery cadence went from two weeks to one, team-health signals improved, and the product↔engineering forum outlived the situation. We shipped the PoC a month ahead of plan.

Reflection

Ambiguity is the cruelest thing you can do to someone who's struggling — clear expectations, early honest feedback, and a documented fair process protect both the person and the team. The honest caveat: a 30-day trial can flatter you — even someone who excels under the spotlight can revert afterward. What I was really assessing wasn't a sprint; it was whether the fit was real.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

At what point did you decide it wasn't going to work?

At a bi-weekly planning. Instead of executing the roadmap we'd agreed, S pulled the team back into re-litigating whether the end goal was even right. That was the tell: not capability — he wouldn't commit to a shared direction, and on a high-stakes release I couldn't have the foundation re-opened every cycle.

So you didn't actually fire anyone — wasn't this just shuffling the problem?

Fair challenge. He wasn't a bad engineer — the context and type of work were wrong for him, and that's a real distinction. The decision was that he couldn't stay on this team, and I owned it; the transfer was the humane execution of that decision, not a way to avoid it. No good landing spot — same call.

How did you handle the conversation and the team afterward?

With transparency. With S: it wasn't about being a weak engineer — it was fit, and part of that was on me for not aligning the team early enough. With the team: I didn't pretend the turbulence hadn't happened; the bar was real and applied consistently — that's what rebuilt trust.

What had you tried before this?

Weekly check-ins with specific observations, lowering the frustration of the peers who'd raised concerns, and real effort into making the roadmap clearer so no engineer — him or a future hire — would operate in the same fog.

What would you do earlier next time?

Invest in alignment during team formation: disagreement is welcome, but it has to be voiced, not acted on silently. And if disagreement is genuinely high, reconsider team structure early — not three planning cycles in.

Delivery notes:
  • Land the beats: diagnosis → explicit documented bar + support → improved on the wrong axes → decisive but humane move → honest cost → team recovered + shipped early.
  • Numbers: cadence 2wk→1wk, PoC 1 month early, transition in 1 month. Lead the Result with the person, then the team.
  • Pre-load two probes: “didn't you just shuffle the problem?” and “wasn't he right to disagree?” — answer both with the person/behavior split.

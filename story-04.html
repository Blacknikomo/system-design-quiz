“Two of my Staff engineers were deadlocked over how to make TeamCity faster — and it had turned personal, to the point it was blocking the whole performance roadmap. I reframed it around shared criteria; we shipped a direction both owned — and both stayed.”

Situation / Task

Customers and competitors were pushing hard on performance — a single instance handled up to ~10k simultaneous jobs, and they wanted well beyond that, with AI-generated code driving load up. Two of my Staff engineers had opposite answers. P had been on the product 20 years and wanted to iteratively optimize internal algorithms and build queues. D was hired specifically to fix performance and wanted to redesign for horizontal scale — multinode, DB sharding, caching. Both credible — but the debate had gone personal: P said D “didn't understand the system's complexity,” D said P “had been in one team too long to see it fresh.” Engineers were taking sides; the roadmap was blocked. My job: unblock performance with a direction we could trust, and not lose either of them.

Action

The same day the tension crossed the professional line, I did 1:1s with each. To P: “Your system knowledge is unbeatable, and I read the heat as you caring about the product. But the market won't wait for gradual gains — we need a qualitative jump, and you're the one person who can guarantee we make it without breaking what customers depend on.” To D, mirrored: “We're a 20-year-old product carrying both genius and outdated decisions — surviving means shedding dead weight, but with thousands of customers we can't do it overnight. That's why I need P's depth and your optimization experience together, not against each other.”

Then I took the ego out by removing the vacuum it was filling. I asked them to pause the debate for a week. I spent two days going as deep as I could into both proposals — I wanted to mediate on the merits, not from the org chart. I came back with a shared decision framework — impact/effort, time, rollback safety, complexity — and ran a 90-minute session where I added a third, product-and-business lens. Because I'd done the homework, I could show I understood the complexity and wasn't taking a side. They accepted the framework, added their own criteria, and we tuned it together. Against those criteria the answer was a hybrid neither had alone: launch D's redesign for the real ceiling, while the legacy patterns change incrementally and safely under P's control.

Result

The conflict never hardened — within weeks the team stopped taking sides, and both Staff engineers went from fighting to co-facilitating the performance discussions. In October 2025 I ran a week-long offline gathering that turned the track into scored Epics — the performance roadmap we still run today. Both engineers stayed; the track went from a “swamp” nobody wanted to a high-reward area that attracts strong engineers; and early work caught a TLS issue on the server-to-DB layer, cutting P99 latency from 6.5s to 1s.

Reflection

With two strong seniors, the job isn't to be the smartest technical voice in the room — it's to build decision criteria they'll both trust. That neutrality is constructed, not given. In any senior-vs-senior deadlock my first question is now “whose criteria are we deciding by?” — before we touch the technical merits.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

What exactly did you say to each of them?

The mirrored 1:1s, close to verbatim: P's depth is the safety guarantee; D's redesign is the ceiling; I need them together, not against each other.

How did you decide who was right?

I didn't pick a person. I made them agree on weighted criteria first — impact/effort, time, rollback safety, complexity — then the direction followed from the criteria, not from whoever pushed hardest.

Whose approach actually won?

D's, on architecture — we launched the redesign, so P's position was slightly weaker. But I deliberately kept P's skin in the game: the 20 years of legacy patterns evolve under his control. That's why it held as a win-win instead of one side losing.

How did you protect against losing one of them?

That was half my mandate from the start. The framing in both 1:1s was that each was essential to the other's success, and giving P ownership of the migration meant the senior whose direction “lost” still had a leading role. Both stayed.

What would you do differently?

Intervene earlier on tone — the signals were visible before the conflict went public. And on the October gathering I kept the invite list lean; a couple of stakeholders felt left out, and I'd widen that next time.

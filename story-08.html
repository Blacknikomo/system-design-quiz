“I was asked to rescue a PoC on a market completely new to us — a month in, all research, zero output, and a competitor had just announced a similar product. Two weeks after I stepped in, the team shipped its first demo.”

Situation / Task

In summer 2025 JetBrains was under market pressure and looking to diversify; agent orchestration looked promising, and entering it meant shipping a few PoCs. My manager staffed a two-pizza team — one senior product manager, one senior engineer, both experienced at launching products — with an extremely vague scope: “build a PoC in this new market.” A month later there was no progress: the team had gone deep into research instead of building. And the window was closing — GitHub had announced a similar product weeks earlier. My manager asked me to get the work organized and take the PoC to release.

Action

First I removed the ambiguity in the assignment itself: I interviewed my manager — what does “the team isn't working” actually mean, what was the original intent? Then I talked to the two people. They were demotivated, and the friction had reached the point where each saw the problem in the other. Experience — and Hanlon's razor — told me the root is usually coordination, not personality. So I reconstructed how they'd worked for the month: no regular syncs from day one, product and engineering visions quietly diverging, no roadmap, no milestones. Root cause: misalignment, not people.

I chose the cheapest way to cut the fog — ultra-short planning instead of hunting a perfect universal design. I explained that aiming straight at an ideal product would be slow and fragile; instead we'd design the system quickly and set a roadmap. I got their buy-in — and, importantly, their authority to act as mediator for a while. Then basic project management, done fast: stakeholder list → agreed global goal for the PoC → broke it into small components → combined them so minimal effort gave maximal user impact → risks → roadmap and release milestones → groomings per component.

Mid-course a real constraint surfaced: a serious frontend gap. I didn't stop the work to wait for hiring — I lined up an outsourcing contract (nontrivial: JetBrains had never worked with outsourcing before), and since legal would take a week, I taught the engineers a frontend intro myself — I'd been a UI engineer — so nothing blocked.

Result

We got a tangible roadmap — an anchor for us and for external stakeholders. The first PoC demo shipped two weeks after I stepped in, and we held that cadence. The company invested: that pair grew into a full standalone team, at which point I stepped away. And the human turn: from blaming each other to collaborating, from underperforming to meets-plus.

Reflection

Hanlon's razor in practice: friction between people is usually a symptom of poor coordination, not incompatibility. Nontrivial resourcing is sometimes the unblock — first outsourcing at JetBrains. And project-management basics matter even for a two-person team. What I'd change: build the channel with my manager earlier — if the team had been set up with a conversation with me and the stakeholders, we wouldn't have lost the month. That link is bidirectional; part of it was mine to build.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

How did you decide where to start with so little clarity?

I didn't try to answer “what to build” first. I removed the uncertainty in the assignment itself — interviewed my manager and both engineers to find the real blocker. Once it was clearly misalignment, the cheapest first step was ultra-short planning: break the goal into components and let fast feedback replace guessing the perfect design.

What did you do when things changed mid-project?

The big change wasn't requirements — it was a surfaced constraint: the frontend gap. I didn't pause for hiring; I ran the outsourcing contract in parallel (a week of legal) and taught the team a frontend intro myself, so we adapted without losing pace.

What if your assumption was wrong?

My key assumption was coordination-not-people. If it had actually been an interpersonal conflict, ultra-planning wouldn't have helped — the plan B was changing the composition, likely one of the two, and restarting with rules agreed up front. But the signals — both could reasonably discuss implementation details — said process.

What would you do differently?

Build the channel with my manager before the team was formed and make the value of transparent planning explicit — that would have saved the lost month. The responsibility for that channel wasn't only his.

How did you reduce ambiguity for the team specifically?

Gave them a concrete “where we're going”: an agreed global goal, broken into components with owners, milestones and a roadmap, prioritized by minimum effort → maximum impact. Out of the fog of “make a PoC” came a list of concrete next steps.

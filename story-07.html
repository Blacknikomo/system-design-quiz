“I took over a team drowning in 30 integrations with 4 engineers and a hard external deadline — and dug it out by deciding, explicitly, what we would stop supporting.”

Situation / Task

Early 2025 I took over a Build Tools Integration team in crisis. Around 30 CI/CD integrations supported by 6 engineers — two already on their way out, dropping us to 4. On top of that, Gradle was deprecating an API we depended on: without action, the newest Gradle would simply stop working with TeamCity. Too much surface, too few people, a hard external deadline. My job was to decide what we'd keep alive — and what we'd consciously let go.

Action

First, I built the priority map myself. The PMs couldn't rank the tools for me, so instead of waiting I triangulated three signals — usage statistics, open-issue volume, and interviews across other departments — and landed on the four integrations that actually carried the business: Maven, Gradle, .NET, and Bazel. I brought the list back to the team to pressure-test; they validated it and added technical nuance, like Gradle's legacy API vs the new Tooling API.

Second, I protected knowledge before it walked out the door — targeted knowledge-sharing from the two leavers into the team, specifically on the critical tools, to break the bus factor.

Third, I made the cut explicit. At a two-day offsite we drew a hard line, and I owned it: the four tools get active development; everything else moves to maintenance mode — critical bugfixes only; genuinely dead integrations (we were still supporting Xcode 4, from 2011) get a formal two-release deprecation; and some I delegated out entirely — to external partners and the community.

Fourth, I attacked the hard constraint directly: I went to Gradle and negotiated a one-year postponement of the API deprecation — so the deadline stopped driving the bus. And I owned it upward: packaged the plan as a “Housekeeping” initiative, presented it to my manager, and held one line — no extra workload on the team, and a dedicated product manager for us. I got both.

Result

By the end of 2025 the housekeeping was done: deprecations executed, non-vital integrations sunset or handed to partners, the Gradle deadline neutralized, dedicated PM coverage secured, and the four critical tools back in active development. It outgrew the team — the maintenance-vs-feature model became how the wider department approaches maintenance. And the human result: a team that had literally called their work a “monkey job” told me in 1:1s it finally felt meaningful.

Reflection

I stand by the calls — the prioritization held up at department scale. My real miss was treating a product-ownership vacuum as something to work around instead of escalating it. I rebuilt priorities from usage data for weeks because no PM owned the question. The lesson isn't “move faster” — it's that an ownership gap is itself the problem to push up on day one, not absorb quietly.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

What exactly did you cut, and how did you decide?

Everything outside Maven, Gradle, .NET and Bazel left active development: maintenance mode for most, formal two-release deprecation for the dead ones, delegation to partners/community where there was a taker. The line came from data — usage, open issues, cross-department interviews — validated by the team, not from taste.

How did the people whose work got cut react?

There were doubts, but the plan was explicit that this was a temporary, reversible focus: maintenance mode isn't deletion, and topics could return after the housekeeping. What sold it was fairness — the same criteria applied to everything — and the visible drop in workload.

Was it the right call in hindsight?

The prioritization — yes, it held and scaled to the department. What I'd change is how long I tolerated the product-ownership vacuum: I should have escalated the missing PM ownership immediately instead of quietly compensating for weeks.

How did you communicate the no?

With the numbers and the plan, not opinion. And where people raised concerns, I listened and folded fixes into the plan — the deprecation timelines, for instance, came out of exactly those conversations.

“My own manager kept spinning up experimental teams by pulling engineers off roadmap work — no owner, no plan — and it was quietly killing our delivery. I disagreed, but it took a few rounds and hard evidence to change how he ran experiments. The operating model we built became the basis for our team restructure.”

Situation / Task

TeamCity is a 20-year-old product, around 70 people. The market had shifted hard — AI, new competitors — new-customer growth was down about 30% and we'd started missing deadlines we could no longer afford. My manager — a strong ex-engineer running the department — wanted to run as many experiments as possible to find traction. I agreed with the goal; I disagreed with the method. He'd take three-to-five engineers who'd never worked together, hand them an abstract problem, and step away — no owner, no plan, no definition of done — while roadmap features got silently postponed. My job, with my peers in Product, Marketing and QA, was to land a shared operating model so experiments didn't burn our delivery.

Action

At first I pushed back purely verbally, and I lost. Early on my manager kept an underperformer I wanted to part ways with; I made the case with documented PIP misses, but he said it was too late and told me to manage the fallout — so I committed and did. That taught me something about him: with a hands-on executor, a verbal argument doesn't move the needle — I needed evidence.

So when he launched three PoC teams — an AI Agents Hub, a new auth subsystem, an IDE integration — I didn't argue in the abstract. I let the pattern surface: within two months two of those teams failed outright and two more stalled, because no one was there to close a discussion and make a call. Then I went to him with exactly that, framed carefully: “The ideas are strong — it's the launch model that's failing us.” He agreed there had to be a better way. Instead of handing him a complaint, I offered to design the fix with him, then widened it into a working group. We landed three things: a Feature Owner role on every PoC team — one person accountable for grooming, planning, the SDLC; a rule that we staff a PoC only after assessing team capacity two-to-four weeks ahead; and a project-management course I organized for the PMs and team leads so we'd all run projects the same way.

And there was a case where the disagreement went the other way. I was convinced we should keep investing in one PoC. My manager pushed back — the opportunity cost was other, more promising bets. He was right. I disagreed in the room, said so, then committed: reallocated the team and backed the call publicly — no second-guessing.

Result

On decision quality: before the change, only one of five experiments ever reached a clean stop — the rest just stalled and quietly drained engineers. After it, we stopped having stalled projects at all: every PoC starts with a definition of ready and done and runs to a clear go/no-go — some became standalone products, some we deliberately shut down instead of letting them rot. The lasting outcome: once cross-team flow was visible, we restructured around it — six vertical functional teams became four stream teams plus two platform teams, the model we still run. The whole arc took about six months. My manager still overrode calls sometimes — but now consciously, weighing the cost.

Reflection

Influencing up isn't always head-on. For a leader who lives in execution, one conversation won't move them — you let the evidence accumulate and bring the pattern, not the opinion. And I should have built the operating model proactively, before the first teams failed.

Likely follow-ups (recall first, then open)

How did you make the case — what evidence?

Not opinion. I let the launch model fail on its own terms — two PoC teams collapsed and two stalled within two months — and brought that pattern plus the root cause: no owner to close discussions and decide. With a hands-on executor, the evidence is what moves them.

What if he still disagreed?

I have a ladder before it becomes a standoff. Make the disagreement cheap to test — one experiment with the new governance, one without, let the data decide. If bigger, bring a neutral read — a peer review or a light audit. And once a call is final, I disagree-and-commit — which I actually did on the PoC I wanted to continue.

How did you behave once a decision was final?

On the PoC I lost: said my piece once, then reallocated the team and backed the call publicly so the team didn't feel the wobble. Later I thanked my manager — we'd built something real together: happier team, finished experiments, a repeatable framework.

Have you ever been wrong in one of these?

Yes — that same PoC. I was sure it deserved more runway; he made the opportunity-cost case. He was right, and I'd have over-invested. That's exactly why disagree-and-commit is a real principle for me, not a slogan.

Wasn't this just going around your manager?

The opposite. Every move was with him — 1:1 first, then co-designing the fix, then widening to the group. I never escalated over him or built a coalition behind his back. The goal was a shared operating model, not winning a point.
